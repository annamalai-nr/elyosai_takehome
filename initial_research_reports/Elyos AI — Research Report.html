1Executive Summary

Elyos AI is a London-based, YC S23-backed company building voice-first AI customer service agents for trades and field services — plumbing, gas/electric, HVAC, fire & security, home services, housing associations. They closed a $13M Series A in January 2026 led by Blackbird Ventures (with Pi Labs, Y Combinator, and 468 Capital participating), bringing total funding to $16.2M. Team is now ~20 people, based at WeWork 145 City Road, London.

Six products are live, all voice-first, all deeply integrated with trades CRMs (Simpro, JobLogic, Commusoft, ServiceTitan, GoHighLevel) and WhatsApp. They claim 15+ customers, 0% churn, 95% CSAT, with marquee accounts like James Frew (handling 500+ calls/day via an Elyos agent), GasCare (all six agents live), and Amax Fire & Security (30% of technical calls fully autonomous).

The take-home you've been given is a 45–90 min CLI exercise (streaming, tool calling, quirky-API discovery). But the on-site explicitly tees up where the product actually lives: real-time voice + concurrent tool calls + scaling to 100 concurrent calls + STT (Deepgram) + TTS (ElevenLabs). The pair-programming session will literally extend your take-home toward voice I/O, concurrent tool calls, and context management. That alignment with the Eugenix CP2 voice agent you've already shipped is the strongest single thing going for you.

The strategic story they're telling is also worth absorbing: they don't see themselves as "AI receptionist for plumbers." They're framing this as the operating system for the AI era of field services, anchored on a "Communication History Management" (CHM) idea — a queryable log of every customer conversation that becomes the new source of truth, eventually replacing the CRM. That gives you a wide canvas for prototype ideas beyond just "another voice bot."

4Product Surface — the six AI Agents

Elyos sells six agent products. All voice-first, all CRM-integrated. Here they are, with each agent's job and your prototype-relevance score:

  • Shape: Inbound, 24/7.
  • Job: Answer after-hours calls, diagnose faults over the phone, transfer emergency call-outs to the on-call engineer, book/reschedule jobs in the FSM, send WhatsApp notifications.

  • Shape: Inbound, fully autonomous, unlimited concurrent calls.
  • Job: General enquiries, book/reschedule jobs, fault diagnosis. "Expertly trained in plumbing and electrics."
  • Customer agent names seen: "Jamie" at James Frew, handling 500+ calls/day, 80% of their daily call volume.
  • Why customers buy it: Handles peak demand without scaling headcount. James Frew's 10,000+ monthly call base ran on a fixed team plus Jamie.
  • Prototype relevance: ★★★★★ — biggest surface area to differentiate on; needs richer integration logic than OOH.

4.3 AI Sales Executive — "Speed to lead"

  • Shape: Outbound + follow-up.
  • Job: Call new leads within seconds of enquiry, qualify, follow up on open quotes, push them through to booking.
  • Customer named: Aquila gave feedback on Sales Agent quality (per the Humans Behind the Agents post).
  • Why customers buy it: UK trades research shows ~50–70% of leads go to whoever responds first; this captures that window.
  • Prototype relevance: ★★★ — different mode (outbound campaigns at scale, retry/queue logic) but well within reach.

4.4 Appointment Confirmation Agent — "Reduce your no-access rate"

  • Shape: Outbound, daily campaigns.
  • Job: Phone every customer in the diary before the engineer dispatches; reschedule if needed; add fresh notes to the job in the FSM.
  • Reported impact: >40% reduction in no-access rates (GasCare).
  • Prototype relevance: ★★★★ — direct match to your Eugenix CP2 outbound post-op flow; cleanest scope.

4.5 Scheduling Assistant — "Scheduling, but with AI"

  • Shape: Outbound + scheduling intelligence.
  • Job: Plan preventative maintenance (PPM) bookings in advance, service renewal reminder calls, optimise the diary by engineer location/skill/customer preference. Can run advisory ("recommend slots") or autonomous.
  • Prototype relevance: ★★ — heavy on optimisation logic; harder to demo crisply in 2 days.

4.6 Field Engineer Assistant — "No more end of day paperwork"

  • Shape: Inbound on a dedicated engineer line. Engineers call IT (i.e., the AI), not the reverse.
  • Job: Update job notes by voice, close jobs, update status, manage timesheets — keep the office phone lines free for customers.
  • Prototype relevance: ★★★ — different audience (the engineer, not the customer). Interesting differentiated angle if you want to surprise them.

Patterns across all six

Every agent has the same pipeline shape: phone (Twilio/SIP) → STT (Deepgram) → LLM with tool calls → TTS (ElevenLabs) → phone, with tools that read/write the customer's FSM. The differentiators are: which direction the call goes, what knowledge/scripts the agent is loaded with, and what tools it can invoke.

This is a critical insight for your prototype: build the underlying pipeline once and the agent role is mostly prompting + tool set + dialog policy. Show them you understand that.

8Strategic Positioning — the "CHM" North Star

The single most important strategic post on their blog is "The CRM of the future isn't a CRM." Read it once before the on-site. The argument:

  • Traditional CRMs try to impose structured fields on inherently unstructured human conversation. They get bloated.
  • A CHM (Communication History Management) system stores the raw conversation history — voice, email, chat, text — and treats that as the source of truth.
  • Once you have that substrate, you can "vibe your CHM" — query it in natural language: "find everyone who sounded unsure at price quote and proactively send a discount."

Why this matters strategically:

  • The voice agents are the data-collection layer. Each call generates structured + unstructured data flowing into the CHM.
  • The CHM is the durable moat. Field service businesses can't easily leave once their entire customer interaction history lives in Elyos.
  • The next products are about querying / acting on that history — proactive outreach, churn detection, customer segmentation, performance analytics.

This reframes what they sell from "AI receptionist" to "communication operating system for trades." Your prototype is a much better fit if you nod to this — e.g., end the call with a structured summary that flows somewhere useful, demonstrate awareness that the call transcript is data.

12The Take-Home Itself — what to lock down

Before you build anything beyond the take-home, nail this. Quick read of what they explicitly score:

Three evaluation criteria, in their own words

  1. Implementation quality — streaming works, both tools work, pending state is clear, cancellation actually cancels, errors handled, code clear in 5 minutes.
  2. API discovery & handling — they say this is "particularly important." They've planted undocumented quirks in the weather + research APIs. Your job is to find them, handle them gracefully, document what you found.
  3. Communication — Loom video clear, you discuss trade-offs, you're honest about weaknesses.

Things to be deliberate about

  • Target size: 150–250 LOC. Past 400 = over-engineering, per the doc.
  • Conversation history maintained across turns. Mentioned in must-haves.
  • Cancellation must actually free resources and return partial results if mid-stream. Mentioned in nice-to-haves but it's the deepest test.
  • Loom video API-discovery section is the most important. Spend disproportionate time on it. They want to see investigative thinking, not polished slides.
  • Include your AI-assistant transcript (/export) if you used Claude Code / Cursor / Copilot. They explicitly want it. Don't hide it.

The "quirks" — what to probe for

They won't tell you, but typical traps in API mocks like this include:

  • Intermittent 5xx / 429 / 500-with-200 mismatches.
  • Variable latency on /research (3–8s stated; might occasionally exceed).
  • Inconsistent response schemas (e.g., location sometimes returns city, sometimes object).
  • Special inputs that crash (empty string, unicode, very long, SQL-injection-style).
  • Returns that look successful but contain garbage / error in body.
  • Rate limits not in the docs.
  • Stale caching on identical queries.
  • Timeouts that hang vs. error.

Probe systematically, document what you found, handle each intentionally.

On the LLM provider

Use OpenAI or Anthropic SDK directly. Don't reach for LangChain/LangGraph — it'll bloat past 400 LOC. Direct SDK + a thin tool loop is what they want to see.

13The Prototype — concrete recommendation

Time budget: weekend (1–2 days). Goal: something polished and crisp that demoes in 5 minutes, not a sprawling system.

Recommended scope

Working title: Streaming voice CLI — same shape as the take-home, but you can talk to it.

Functionality:

  1. Speak into your laptop mic → Deepgram streaming STT.
  2. Streamed transcript → LLM (OpenAI or Anthropic) with tool calling.
  3. Two tools wired up:
    • get_weather (same endpoint they gave you).
    • lookup_customer_history — calls their /research endpoint (treat it as your slow "CHM lookup"). 3–8 second pending state.
  4. TTS streaming back via ElevenLabs while LLM is responding.
  5. Cancellation: spoken keyword ("stop", "cancel", "wait") or Ctrl-C interrupts the in-flight slow tool, returns to listening, preserves partial response.
  6. Pending state: an audible "let me look that up for you, one moment" while research is in flight (this is what voice-agent customers expect; the silent-pause UX in CLI is unacceptable in voice).
  7. Concurrent calls: structure the code so a second concurrent "call" (a second mic source, or a simulated WebSocket) can run alongside — even if you don't fully wire two real mics, prove it architecturally.
  8. Tiny CHM log: every utterance gets logged as {call_id, timestamp, speaker, transcript, tool_calls, sentiment_guess} to a local JSON file. After the call, demo a one-line query command: python query.py "calls where the user cancelled" → returns the matching entries.

Stack to use (you already know all of this)

Python 3.12 (asyncio throughout)
deepgram-sdk (streaming STT)
elevenlabs (streaming TTS)
openai or anthropic (LLM with tools)
sounddevice / pyaudio (mic capture)
fastapi (optional — for concurrent call simulation via WebSocket)
